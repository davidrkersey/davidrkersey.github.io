Assessing Medicare Payouts¶

Last week President Donald Trump released his budget proposal for the federal government for the coming year. His initial proposal includes a considerable cut, USD 451 billion and USD 920 billion, to Medicare and Medicaid respectively, over the next 10 years. Medicare is estimated to provide health insurance to over 60 million Americans, most of who are over the age of 65. Roughly 14% of the federal budget in FY18 was spent on Medicare, which amounts to approximately USD 582 billion.

As the medical industry weighs the future of medical insurance in the US, it will be pertinent to identify revenue streams that are buoyed by national health insurance programs such as Medicare and Medicaid and prepare their business strategy for budget cuts. The following project ranks total annual, national Medicare payouts by physician practice type for the year 2016.

The Data¶

The data is a CSV file from Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS). Each instance is the annual aggregate data for a physician who charged Medicare for the year 2016. The original data includes numerous variables describing the physician, as well as variables detailing their financial interaction with Medicare (67 columns in total). I reduced the dimensions by deleting columns I would not be using in my analysis. I used the following columns: Physician ID, Physician practice type (type of medicine), and Total Payout (made to that physician by Medicare) in 2016. from mrjob.job import MRJob
from mrjob.step import MRStep

class Rank_P_type(MRJob):
    
    def steps(self):
          return  [
              MRStep(mapper = self.mapper_choose,
                     reducer = self.reducer_agg),
              MRStep(reducer = self.reducer_sort)]
                    
    
    def mapper_choose(self,_,line):
        (NPID, P_type, tot_pay) = line.split(',')
        yield P_type, float(tot_pay)
    
        
    def reducer_agg(self, P_type, total):
        for i in total:
            if P_type == P_type:
                p_list = list(total)
                yield None, (round(sum(p_list)), P_type, len(p_list), round(sum(p_list)/len(p_list)), round(min(p_list)), round(max(p_list)))
    
    def reducer_sort(self, _, value):
        for tot, P_type, count, avg_p, min_p, max_p in sorted(value, reverse=True):
            yield (P_type, count), ("$"+format(tot), "$"+format(min_p), "$"+format(avg_p), "$"+format(max_p))
                     
            
         
        
if __name__ == '__main__':
    Rank_P_type.run()

Overwriting IA_BigData_Part3.py

My code contains one mapper and two reducers, which is not a very extensive MapReduce program, but the project was for demonstration purposes. The mapper reads in the data line by line and applies an alias to each variable of the data. From that mapper I yield the key, value pair of (physician type), (float of total payout). Next, my first reducer essentially groups each instance by physician type and puts those instances into a list. I then use the functionality of a list to perform various calculations by physician type, such as total payout, number of physicians, and min, max, average payouts. The output for this reducer is a key, value pair of Null, (tuple containing all my data). Finally, my second reducer reads the null key value and discards it. It reads the tuple value and unpacks it while sorting it by total payout (the metric I wanted to rank physician types by) and yields a key of (physician type, number of physicians in that type), (all the measurements).

Output¶

The output is formatted as follows:

(Physician Type, Number of Physicians), (Total Medicare Payout, Min Payout Made, Average Payout Made, Max Payout Made)

They are ordered descending by Total Medicare Payout.

Analysis¶

My analysis indicates that 948,189 practices, spread across 90 different practice types, charged Medicare in 2016. Internal medicine practices received the highest total annual payout from Medicare, with a total of USD 6.68 billion. The max annual payout any single practice received was USD 208 million by a clinical laboratory. This data point might be of interest to taxpayers, or anybody tasked with investigating Medicare fraud, as it far exceeds the next closest (~USD 84 million for a portable X-ray supplier). Additionally, clinical laboratories received the highest average annual payout among all practice types (USD 1.61 million), however, that is distorted by the single max payout mentioned above and further proves the point of how much of an outlier that data point may be. Radiation therapy centers were second in average annual payout at USD 1.18 million with a considerably modest max annual payout of USD 7,248,542.

Additional analysis could use the same data source to break down the analysis from a national level to a state and zip code level. This could allow various businesses in the medical industry to prepare for regional changes in revenue as the budget cuts are enacted.

Conclusion¶

Finally, after exhaustive research into how to unpack ‘generator’ tuples I discovered that it was easier for me to use the data by turning the tuples into a list, as I do in my first reducer. This allowed me to freely index the data for each physician type and execute the calculations for my analysis.

While MapReduce is largely outdated, it was valuable to learn the methodology inside-out by writing my own program to address a business problem. Additionally, this was my first object-oriented programming experience. I look forward to working in Spark in the near future.
